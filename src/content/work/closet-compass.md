## What this taught me about product

**Distribution is half the product.** Closet Compass shipped a working AI in 200 hours but spent most of the next four months on micro-influencer partnerships, campus activations, organic Instagram, and getting users to keep using it. The product mattered. So did the path users took to it.

**Customer discovery is not validation.** Through NSF I-Corps we ran 30+ structured interviews with people who *would obviously want this*. They all said yes. Then we built a beta and watched what they actually did. The gap between stated and revealed preference is where most consumer startups die. The DAU number (~40%) was the one that mattered — not the waitlist number.

**Sustainability has to pay out for the user, not just the planet.** Lots of "buy less, wear more" pitches; almost none monetize correctly because the user doesn't feel the savings. We tried freemium with premium styling insights and affiliate partnerships — affiliate was tricky because it pulled toward "buy this new thing," which contradicts the thesis. This is unfinished work.

## The exit

I built Closet Compass while finishing my final year at UC Davis. Seven days before my flight back to India after graduation, I met the CEO of nSpire AI at an AI conference (I went to *every* AI conference that fall — read [the side-door PM post](/blog/side-door-pm/) for the longer version). We talked about what I'd shipped, what users had taught me, why I was obsessed with product.

The interview happened the next week. The acqui-hire offer came the day after.

One week I was a confused founder. The next, a PM in Silicon Valley with a backpack, a hotel room, and zero plan. The Closet Compass team joined nSpire to scale the platform. The styling problem is still right — it's just on a longer roadmap than I thought.

## What I'd do differently

I'd spend less time on the AI tagging accuracy in the first six weeks and more time on the *recommendation feedback loop*. The tagging was technically impressive at 95%+ accuracy. The recommendations were mediocre because we didn't have enough wear-data per user. We optimized for the impressive demo metric instead of the metric that compounds with usage.

A version-two would invert: start with three outfits hand-curated by a human stylist on day one, capture wear/skip signal on every recommendation, and only train the AI once you have ~200 wear-decisions per user. Cold-start was the real problem; tagging was a distraction.
